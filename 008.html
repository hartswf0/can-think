SLIDE 1/15

PLATFORMS FROM TURING'S
'COMPUTING MACHINERY AND INTELLIGENCE'
(1950)

A College-Level Lecture on the Foundations of AI
Alan M. Turing | Mind 59:236 | October 1950

HISTORICAL CONTEXT

  • Published: Mind, October 1950
  • Question: "Can machines think?"
  • Turing's Strategy: Replace metaphysical question with operational test
  • Manchester Mark 1 computer operational (165,000 binary digits storage)
  • Prediction: By 2000, machines will pass imitation game (70% success in 5 minutes)

PLATFORM 1: THE IMITATION GAME AS TEST

Key Concept:

"Replacing 'Can machines think?' with operational behavioral test"

Example:

Three-person game - interrogator (C), man (A), woman (B) communicate through text only

Argument:

Test whether machine can imitate human responses to point of indistinguishability

"Instead of attempting such a definition I shall replace the question by another, which is closely related to it and is expressed in relatively unambiguous words."

Key Features:

Behavioral Empirical Avoids metaphysics Performance-focused
Course Connection: Intelligence as media problem (W10S20)

PLATFORM 2: THE UNIVERSAL MACHINE

Key Concept:

"Digital computers capable of mimicking any discrete-state machine"

Example:

Manchester machine with 165,000 binary digits storage

Argument:

Because computers are universal, questions about intelligence reduce to programming questions

"They can mimic any discrete-state machine - described as universal machines"

Key Features:

Programmable Universal Discrete-state Deterministic
Course Connection: Computation as substrate-independent (W13S26)

PLATFORM 10: THE LEARNING MACHINE AS CHILD

Key Concept:

"Program a child-like machine and educate it rather than directly programming adult mind"

Example:

Child's brain as "notebook with little mechanism, lots of blank sheets"

Argument:

More tractable to create simple child machine and educate it than program all complexity

"Instead of trying to produce a programme to simulate the adult mind, why not rather try to produce one which simulates the child's?"

Key Features:

Educational Evolutionary Reward-punishment based Gradual complexity
Course Connection: Technogenesis - human-machine co-evolution (W10S19)

PLATFORM 12: TEACHER IGNORANCE & EMERGENT BEHAVIOR

Key Concept:

"Learning machine's internal states become opaque even to its teacher"

Example:

Manchester program (1,000 units) produces unpredictable responses to 16-figure numbers in 2 seconds

Argument:

"Machines can only do what we order them" is strange - most programs produce unpredictable behavior

"An important feature of a learning machine is that its teacher will often be very largely ignorant of quite what is going on inside"

Key Features:

Emergent complexity Teacher opacity Natural uncertainty Learning-induced unpredictability
Course Connection: AI alignment crisis - opacity at scale (W13S25)

PLATFORM 15: THE TELEPRINTER AS EPISTEMIC EQUALIZER

Key Concept:

"Communication medium as arbiter of what counts as intelligence"

Example:

Teleprinter eliminates voice tone, physical presence, all non-textual cues

Argument:

Choice of communication medium fundamentally shapes what we can test and therefore know

"In order that tones of voice may not help... the ideal arrangement is to have a teleprinter communicating between the two rooms"

Key Features:

Medium-determined epistemology Text privilege Selective sensory deprivation
Course Connection: Reading with machines - cognitive mediation (W10S19)

PLATFORM 16: THE GENDER GAME AS MISDIRECTION

Key Concept:

"Original game involves gender imitation, not human-machine distinction"

Example:

Man (A) tries to make interrogator (C) wrongly identify him as woman (B)

Argument:

By first establishing game about imitating gender (already socially constructed category), machine substitution becomes less radical

"We now ask the question, 'What will happen when a machine takes the part of A in this game?'"

Key Features:

Performance-based ontology Social construction Strategic misdirection Provocative politics
Course Connection: Diegetic prototypes - what futures argued into being? (W11S21)

PLATFORM 18: THE SUPERCRITICAL MIND

Key Concept:

"Minds as nuclear piles that either dampen or amplify incoming ideas"

Example:

Most minds subcritical (<1 idea per input); some supercritical (cascading ideas into theories)

Argument:

Question isn't just "can machines think?" but "can machines be supercritical?"

"The majority seem to be 'subcritical'... An idea presented will on average give rise to less than one idea in reply. A smallish proportion are supercritical."

Key Features:

Threshold effects Amplification vs dampening Phase transitions Physics-inspired cognitive model
Course Connection: Nuclear metaphor as speculative design fiction (W11S21)

PLATFORM 27: THE CONSTITUTION ANALOGY

Key Concept:

"Distinguishing time-invariant machine rules from ephemeral learned rules"

Example:

Like U.S. Constitution - meta-rules for changing ordinary laws while Constitution remains stable

Argument:

Paradox of learning ("How can rules change?") dissolves with hierarchical rule systems

"The rules which get changed in the learning process are of a rather less pretentious kind, claiming only an ephemeral validity. The reader may draw a parallel with the Constitution of the United States."

Key Features:

Hierarchical rule systems Meta-rules vs object-rules Constitutional model Stable architecture enabling flexible behavior
Course Connection: Cybernetic systems - feedback and self-modification (W12S24)

PLATFORM 29: THE PROPHETIC VOICE PROBLEM

Key Concept:

"Social and rhetorical challenge of expressing beliefs about future AI capabilities"

Example:

By century's end, people will speak of machines thinking "without expecting to be contradicted"

Argument:

Making predictions serves scientific progress even when uncertain; conjectures suggest useful research directions

"I believe that at the end of the century the use of words and general educated opinion will have altered so much that one will be able to speak of machines thinking without expecting to be contradicted. I believe further that no useful purpose is served by concealing these beliefs."

Key Features:

Performative prophecy Justification of speculation Research program advocacy Self-aware futurism
Course Connection: Hyperstition - fictions that make themselves real (W12S23)

PLATFORM 33: THE WASTEPAPER BASKET AS LABOR TRANSFORMATION

Key Concept:

"Shift from direct programming to machine learning represents fundamental transformation in cognitive labor"

Example:

"60 workers over 50 years couldn't program adult intelligence 'if nothing went into the wastepaper basket'"

Argument:

Human labor shifts from direct production to curation of evolutionary processes - the basket is where the real work happens

"At my present rate of working I produce about a thousand digits of programme a day... if nothing went into the wastepaper basket. Some more expeditious method seems desirable."

Key Features:

Shift from production to curation Iterative failure as primary work Evolutionary selection as labor
Course Connection: Concrete cognitive/labor shift with AI scaling (W10S19)

PLATFORM 37: THE VIVA VOCE AS CONSCIOUSNESS TEST

Key Concept:

"Imitation game parallels oral examination detecting presence of authentic comprehension versus ghost of mere reproduction"

Example:

Sonnet viva voce testing whether understanding is "real" or "learnt parrot fashion"

Argument:

Test tries to distinguish living comprehension from undead, authentic thought from zombie simulacrum

"The game (with player B omitted) is frequently used in practice under the name of viva voce to discover whether some one really understands something or has 'learnt it parrot fashion.'"

Key Features:

Hauntological testing Presence vs ghost Authentic vs simulated understanding Strategic imperfection
Course Connection: Freud's uncanny, Fisher's crackle, hauntings in AI (W12S23)

PLATFORM 40: THE ALIGNMENT PROBLEM

Key Concept:

"Turing predicts the central problem of contemporary AI: we won't understand systems we create, yet will be responsible for their behavior"

Example:

"Teacher will be 'very largely ignorant of quite what is going on inside' the learning machine"

Argument:

How do we ensure AI systems pursue human values when we can't understand their internal workings?

"This is in clear contrast with normal procedure when using a machine to do computations: one's object is then to have a clear mental picture of the state of the machine at each moment in the computation."

Key Features:

Constitutive opacity Alignment-through-ignorance Black-box responsibility Pedagogical uncertainty
Course Connection: "Machines of Loving Grace" vs alignment realities (W13S25)

TURING'S LEGACY: FROM 1950 TO TODAY

Predicted by 2000: Machines would pass imitation game with 70% success in 5 minutes

Reality 2025: LLMs approach but don't reliably pass rigorous Turing Tests

Core Insights Still Relevant:

  • Intelligence as media/performance problem
  • Learning machines require opacity and emergent behavior
  • Human-machine co-evolution (technogenesis)
  • Alignment problem inherent to opaque learning systems
  • Hyperstition: Turing's prophecy shaped 75 years of AI development
Question Remains: "Can machines think?" or "What kind of conversation counts as intelligence?"
